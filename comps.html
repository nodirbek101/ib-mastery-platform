The workhorse multiple
◆ The big idea

Pick a set of comparable public companies, spread their multiples (EV/EBITDA, P/E, …), then apply a chosen multiple to the target's own metric to get an implied value. Rosenbaum & Pearl, Ch. 1

I

Select the universe of comparable companies

The foundation of comps is a well-chosen peer set. Strong comparables share both a business profile (sector, products, end-markets, distribution) and a financial profile (size, growth, margins, returns). The closer the match, the more meaningful the multiple.

You build the list from competitors named in filings, the peer groups equity-research analysts use, and screens by industry code and size. For ValueCo we land on 15 specialty/commodity chemical companies, grouped into tiers.

◆ Garbage in, garbage out

A sloppy universe is the most common way to get comps wrong. A pure-play peer in the same niche is worth more than five loosely-related names. Ch. 1, Step I

II

Locate the necessary financial information

For each company you gather the data needed to compute multiples: the latest SEC filings (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K), consensus estimates for forward years, the current share price and share count, and market data. Public companies make this straightforward; the discipline is consistency.

A key step is building a last-twelve-months (LTM) figure so every company is measured over the same trailing year regardless of its fiscal year-end:

Calendarisation — LTM
The "stub" is the partial-year period; adding the current stub and removing the prior stub rolls the figure forward to today.
III

Spread key statistics, ratios & trading multiples

"Spreading" means computing each company's value and multiples on a consistent basis. First, two value measures:

Equity value & enterprise value
Enterprise value is capital-structure-neutral — the value of the operating business to all investors.

Then the multiples. EV multiples use whole-firm metrics; equity multiples use per-share metrics:

The core trading multiples
EV/EBITDA is the workhorse — neutral to capital structure and depreciation policy. Reported figures are first adjusted for non-recurring items so multiples reflect ongoing performance.
◆ Match the numerator to the denominator

EV pairs with EBITDA, EBIT and Sales (pre-financing). Equity value (and price) pairs with net income and EPS (post-financing). Mixing them is a classic error. Ch. 1, Step III

IV

Benchmark the comparable companies

With every company spread, you lay them side by side and study the dispersion — growth, margins, returns (ROIC/ROE), leverage and coverage. This tells you which peers are the truest comparables and where the target should sit within the range.

Bankers usually lead with the median (robust to outliers) and also show mean, high and low to frame a range. A target that is higher-growth and lower-levered than the median peer deserves a multiple above the median.

◆ Judgement, not just arithmetic

Benchmarking is where the analyst earns their keep — deciding which names to weight and what multiple the target merits. Ch. 1, Step IV

V

Determine valuation

Finally, apply the chosen multiple to the target's own metric to get an implied value, and bridge from enterprise to equity:

Implied valuation
For ValueCo: 7.3× × $700m LTM EBITDA ≈ $5.1bn EV; less $1,317m net debt ≈ $3.8bn equity. The low/high multiples give a range.
