◆ The big idea

Project the target's unlevered free cash flow for ~5 years, estimate a terminal value for everything after, and discount it all to today at the WACC. The sum is enterprise value. Rosenbaum & Pearl, Ch. 3, pp. 109–112

I

Study the target & its key drivers

Every valuation starts with diligence. Before a single number goes into a cell, you study the company and its sector to understand what actually drives performance — sales growth, profitability, and free-cash-flow generation. Shortcuts here cause distortions everywhere downstream.

For a public company, you mine SEC filings (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K), equity research, earnings-call transcripts, and investor presentations. For a private target, you rely on management materials — in an M&A process, typically a Confidential Information Memorandum (CIM).

◆ Our target — ValueCo

Throughout, we value ValueCo Corporation: 2019 sales of $3,450m, a 40% gross margin, and a healthy ~22% EBITDA margin. These are the figures you'll turn into a valuation in the Lab.

II

Project free cash flow

The core of a DCF is unlevered free cash flow (FCF) — the cash a company generates after operating expenses, taxes, capital expenditures and working-capital needs, but before any interest. It's "unlevered" because it's the cash available to all capital providers, independent of how the company is financed.

Unlevered Free Cash Flow
Start from EBIT, tax it (giving EBIAT), add back non-cash D&A, subtract the real cash costs of capex and working-capital growth.

Each line is built from an assumption applied to projected sales:

  • Sales grows off a chosen rate each year — ValueCo: 7.5% → 6% → 5% → 4% → 3%, gliding toward a steady state.
  • COGS at 60% of sales → a 40% gross margin.
  • SG&A at ~19% of sales. Gross profit − SG&A = EBITDA.
  • D&A at 6% of sales — subtracted to find EBIT, then added back (non-cash).
  • Capex at 4.5% of sales, and the increase in net working capital, both real cash uses.
◆ Why tax EBIT, not pre-tax income?

Unlevered FCF deliberately ignores interest expense. So we apply tax to EBIT, not to earnings after interest. The value of debt's tax shield is captured separately — inside the WACC. Ch. 3, pp. 110–111, 141–149

III

Calculate the WACC

The weighted average cost of capital is the rate used to discount future cash flows to today. It blends the required returns of debt and equity holders, weighted by the target's capital structure — a single number that reflects the business's risk.

Weighted Average Cost of Capital
kd = cost of debt, ke = cost of equity (via CAPM), t = tax rate, D/V & E/V = debt and equity weights.
Cost of Equity — Capital Asset Pricing Model
rf = risk-free rate, β = levered beta, ERP = equity risk premium. ValueCo: 2.5% + 1.43 × 7.0% + 1.1% ≈ 13.6%.

Cost of equity comes from the Capital Asset Pricing Model: risk-free rate + β × equity risk premium (+ a size premium). For ValueCo the build-up gives a cost of equity of ~13.6% and an after-tax cost of debt of ~4.9%, which at a 30/70 debt/equity mix blends to a WACC of 11.0%.

◆ Why it matters so much

WACC discounts every future dollar. A small change moves the valuation a lot — which is exactly why the DCF output is a range, sensitised on WACC, not a single number. Ch. 3, pp. 111, 150–156

IV

Determine terminal value

We can't forecast forever, so a terminal value captures the company's worth beyond the explicit period — its "going-concern" value. It typically accounts for the majority of total DCF value, so the terminal year must reflect a normalised steady state, not a cyclical peak or trough.

Two accepted methods:

Exit Multiple Method (used here)
ValueCo: $929.2m × 7.5× = $6,969m, anchored to where comparable companies trade.
Perpetuity Growth Method (the cross-check)
Treats terminal-year FCF as a perpetuity growing at g forever. The 7.5× exit multiple implies g ≈ 2.6% — a sensible long-run rate, which validates the assumption.
◆ Always reconcile the two

Bankers compute terminal value with the exit multiple, then back out the implied perpetuity growth rate to sanity-check it. If the implied g were 6%, the multiple would be too aggressive. Ch. 3, pp. 111, 157–160

V

Present value & determine valuation

Finally, discount each year's FCF and the terminal value back to today using the discount factor, and sum them to get enterprise value.
